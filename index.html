September 3rd, 2025

Omission of Section 13(8)(b) of IGST Act: Impact on Indian and Foreign Intermediaries

Background

Section 13(8)(b) of the IGST Act currently specifies that for intermediary services, the place of supply is deemed to be the location of the service provider. This creates complications in cross-border transactions involving intermediaries (like brokers, agents, commission agents, sale reps and alike etc) resulting in tax challenges and disputes.

Recommended Change

The 56th GST Council proposed the omission of Section 13(8)(b), so the place of supply for intermediary services will now be the location of the service recipient. This amendment aligns the law with the destination principle of GST.

Impact on Indian Intermediaries

  • Indian intermediaries supplying services to foreign clients will now have the place of supply deemed as outside India (location of recipient).
  • These intermediary services will be classified as export of services for GST purposes.
  • Exports are zero-rated, so Indian intermediaries will not pay GST on such supplies.
  • They will be eligible to claim input tax credit (ITC) and refunds on GST paid on inputs / input services / capital goods (if any) used to supply these services.
  • This change improves competitiveness for Indian service providers as intermediaries in global markets and provides much-needed tax relief.

Impact on Foreign Intermediaries

  • Foreign intermediaries providing services to Indian recipients were earlier not subject to GST because the place of supply was their own foreign location.
  • Post-omission, the place of supply will be India (location of the recipient).
  • Such services will be classified as "import of services" and attract GST under reverse charge mechanism payable by Indian recipients.
  • Indian recipients will pay GST but can claim input tax credit or refunds (in case of zero rated supplies), ensuring tax neutrality.
  • This means foreign intermediaries' services to India will be taxable but fully creditable/refundable within the Indian GST system.

Effective Date

  • The amendment proposal was accepted in the 56th GST Council meeting.
  • The proposed amendment required amendment in law and hence would require Parliament's approval followed by President assent and subsequent notification for effective date of implementation.

Refunds

A. Sanction of Provisional Refund in case of Zero-Rated Supplies under LUT

Purpose

This amendment aims to facilitate and expedite refund claims for zero-rated supplies while incorporating a risk-based assessment system to mitigate fraudulent claims. The detailed scrutiny option preserves the authority's ability to thoroughly check claims when necessary.

Mechanism

The (GST) Council has recommended an amendment in rule 91(2) of the CGST Rules, 2017 regarding the sanction of provisional refunds related to zero-rated supplies, which include export of goods or services or supplies to a Special Economic Zone (SEZ) developer/unit for authorized operations.

Key Points

  • Provisional refund will be sanctioned to the extent of 90% of the refund claimed by the registered person.
  • The legal provision are already contained in Section 54(6) of the CGST Act 2017. However the Rule 91 of the CGST Rules 2017 was not aligned.
  • The sanction of this provisional refund will be done by the proper officer based on the identification and evaluation of risk by a system.
  • In exceptional cases, the proper officer may choose, with reasons recorded in writing, to bypass provisional refund and proceed with detailed scrutiny of the refund claim.
  • A notification will be issued to specify certain categories of registered persons who will not be eligible for provisional refunds.

Effective Date

  • These provisions will come into effect from November 1, 2025.

B. Sanction of Provisional Refund in case of Inverted Duty Structure (IDS)

Purpose

To improve the timely disbursal of refund claims related to IDS by facilitating taxpayers claiming refunds on account of inverted duty structure to be eligible for provisional refund of 90% of the claimed amount subject to risk-based evaluation by GST authorities, even before the amendment to the law is formally done.

Background

Currently, under Section 54(6) of the CGST Act, 2017, provisional refunds of 90% of the claimed amount are allowed for zero-rated supplies. However, such provisional refunds are not explicitly extended to refunds arising from the inverted duty structure (IDS).

Proposal by the GST Council

To amend Section 54(6) of the CGST Act to allow sanction of 90% of the refund claimed on a provisional basis in cases arising out of inverted duty structure, similar to the treatment for zero-rated supply refunds.

Government Decision for stop-gap arrangement

Pending formal amendments to the CGST Act, the Central Government has directed Central Board of Indirect Taxes and Customs (CBIC) to issue instructions to Central Tax field formations for granting provisional refunds of 90% of the claimed amount arising out of IDS refunds.

Mechanism

The provisional refunds will be granted based on risk identification and evaluation by the system, similar to the existing process followed for provisional refunds on zero-rated supplies.

Effective Date

This operational procedure will come into effect from 1 November 2025.

C. Allowing Refunds of IGST paid in case of Low Value Export Consignment

Issue

Currently, under Section 54(14) of the CGST Act, 2017, there exists a threshold limit of Rs. 1000 for claiming GST refunds on exports made with payment of tax. This provision poses a challenge for small exporters who make exports via low-value consignments, such as courier or postal shipments, where refund amounts often fall below this threshold. As a result, these exporters face difficulty in claiming timely and full GST refunds, impacting their cash flow and international competitiveness.

Proposed Recommendation for Amendment in Law

  • The GST Council has recommended an amendment to remove the threshold limit in Section 54(14) for refunds arising from exports made with payment of tax.
  • There will no longer be a minimum claim amount requirement for refund of GST on export consignments.
  • Refunds will become accessible for all export transactions regardless of refund amount, enabling small exporters and MSMEs using courier or postal modes to claim refunds without restriction.
  • The amendment aims to facilitate ease of doing business, improve cash flow for smaller exporters, and boost their participation in cross-border trade.

Effective Date

  • The amendment proposal was accepted in the 56th GST Council meeting.
  • The proposed amendment required amendment in law and hence would require Parliament's approval followed by President assent and subsequent notification for effective date of implementation.

Post Sale Discounts & Supply by Dealer to Manufacturers

A. Law Changes in Section 15(3) and Section 34

Background of the Issue

The issue with the existing provisions of the CGST Act related to post-sale discounts arises primarily from the stringent conditions imposed under Section 15(3)(b)(i). Currently, to deduct a post-sale discount from the value of supply for GST purposes, the discount must be established by a pre-supply agreement and specifically linked to invoices. This has created practical difficulties for businesses, as post-sale discounts are often given after the supply is completed and may not always have a formal pre-agreed condition tied to invoices, leading to ambiguity and disputes.

The problem is compounded by the requirement under Section 15(3)(b)(ii) that the recipient must reverse the Input Tax Credit (ITC) attributable to the discount. Ensuring compliance with this provision has been administratively complex, requiring mechanisms such as Circular No. 212/6/2024-GST, which imposed burdens on both suppliers and recipients to prove ITC reversal, thus creating recurring compliance difficulties and disputes.

Proposed Changes as Recommended by GST Council

The change proposed by the GST Council includes:

  • Omission of Section 15(3)(b)(i) to remove the prerequisite of a pre-supply agreement and explicit invoice linking for granting post-sale discounts. This simplification aligns the law more closely with commercial realities where discounts may be decided after supply.
  • Amendment of Section 15(3)(b) to mandate that discounts must be granted through a credit note issued under Section 34. This formalizes the post-sale discount through a standardized instrument (credit note), ensuring transparency and better audit trails.
  • Corresponding amendment of Section 34 to explicitly refer to Section 15(3)(b), making it clear that when a credit note reduces the value of supply, the recipient must reverse the proportional ITC. This eliminates ambiguity over ITC reversal.
  • Rescission of Circular No. 212/6/2024-GST that imposed compliance mechanisms for ITC reversal, as the amendments will provide a clearer, streamlined framework.

Effective Date

  • The amendment proposal was accepted in the 56th GST Council meeting.
  • The proposed amendment required amendment in law and hence would require Parliament's approval followed by President assent and subsequent notification for effective date of implementation.

Currently there are plethora of SCN, Orders etc which are covering this issue of discounts received by the taxpayers by way of commercial credit notes. Anti Profiteering

Anti-Profiteering under GST: Legal Framework, Industry Impact, and Judicial Insights

Parliament, while introducing GST, inserted a unique consumer-protection mechanism: the anti-profiteering provision under Section 171 of the CGST Act, 2017.

The essence of this provision is simple but stringent: if the government reduces the GST rate on goods or services, or extends the benefit of ITC, such benefit must be passed on to the recipient by way of a commensurate reduction in price.

With the 56th GST Council historic deliberation on rate rationalisation has been done and industries are again anxious.

As GST rates have reduced, businesses are wondering whether they may come under renewed scrutiny for profiteering as past experience shows that even inadvertent failures to update MRPs, contracts, or tariffs have led to severe penalties.

Statutory Basis

Section 171(1) of the CGST Act mandates:

"Any reduction in the rate of tax on any supply of goods or services or the benefit of input tax credit shall be passed on to the recipient by way of commensurate reduction in prices."

Sub-section (2) authorised the constitution of an authority to monitor compliance, while sub-section (3A) provided for penalties in the case of contravention.

The provision is intentionally broad. It does not prescribe a fixed methodology for determining "commensurate reduction," leaving room for disputes but also enabling flexibility across industries.

Sunset Clause on Anti-Profiteering

Importantly, a sunset clause from April 1, 2025 now prevents new complaints, though pending cases continue.

Whether Anti Profiteering provisions have been brought back?

No, Anti Profiteering have not been brought back. The industry is expected to decide prices basis the demand and supply. The previous experience of Government suggests that Industry had passed on the benefit of the reduced GST Rates. Thus greater reliance has been placed on the self-regulation only.

Challenges due to "GenZ" GST Reform

  • a. Duty Inversion Increases due to which the IDS refund cases increases. But there is no refund of input services and capital goods. It creates the position of blocking of working capital
  • b. Situation of shifting goods / services from taxable to exempted category breaks the flow of ITC and due to this reason the complete benefit of reduced GST Rate is not passed to the customer as the ITC which was earlier creditable now becomes a cost
  • c. The refunds under automatic route for export of goods reduced in case of reduce in rate of GST on goods. Now due to said reason the refund of accumulated ITC under LUT mode may increase. But that hampers the calculation of refund under inverted duty structure.
  • d. Due to change in rate of tax on goods, now with reduced rates, the situation of refund of ITC accumulation due to inverted duty arises. However due to para 3 of circular no 135/05/2020 dated 31.03.2020 is not allowed. Same is in contradiction of decision in case of M/s BMG Informatics Pvt Ltd vs UOI- 2021-TIOL-1831-HC-Gaw-GST. Thus it leads to blocking of working capital and litigation.
  • e. Demands for goods have dried and the movement of goods in transit has stopped and fresh orders are also on hold in all those cases where GST Rates are recommended to be reduced with effect from 22nd September 2025. Time of Supply and Change in Rate Scenarios

Section 14 of the CGST Act applies when there is a change in the rate of GST. The section compares three critical dates: Date of Supply, Date of Invoice, and Date of Payment.

Supply Before Rate Change

  • Invoice & payment after rate change: New rate applies
  • Invoice before, payment after rate change: Old rate applies
  • Payment before, invoice after change: Old rate applies

Supply After Rate Change

  • Invoice & payment before change: Old rate applies
  • Invoice before, payment after change: New rate applies
  • Payment before, invoice after change: New rate applies

Note

Payment date shall be date of credit in the bank account or date of entry in Books of Account, whichever is earlier, with a margin of atleast 4 days from the date of rate change.

Practical Guidance for Businesses

  • Freeze billing windows before rate changes, built Section 14 logic into ERP systems;
  • Track advances received for supplies spanning rate cuts, adjust old-rate advances against new-rate liability. Section 34 credit notes or refunds?
  • Circulate clear rate-change scenarios to finance, sales, and billing teams.

Discountenance of Compensation Cess

Compensation Cess Discontinuation:

Compensation Cess will be discontinued on all goods except tobacco and tobacco-related products from September 22, 2025.

Items Continuing to Attract Compensation Cess:

  • Cigarettes
  • Gutkha
  • Pan Masala
  • Chewing tobacco (zarda, unmanufactured tobacco)
  • Beedi

Compensation Cess on these items will remain until government loans taken during the pandemic to compensate states for revenue shortfall are fully repaid.

Items Where Compensation Cess Will Discontinue:

Compensation Cess will be removed for the following goods and others from the effective date:

  • Automobiles (including cars over certain engine capacity and dimension)
  • Aerated drinks, soft drinks & Caffeinated & Carbonated Beverages
  • Coal and Lignite
  • Yacht, Other Vessel for pleasure and Sports
  • Luxury and sin goods other than tobacco products

Revised GST Rates:

For goods where Compensation Cess is discontinued, only the revised GST rates (5% or 18% or 40%) will apply from September 22, 2025.

Treatment of Balance in Compensation Cess ECL:

Currently, no provision exists in the law on how the unused balance of Compensation Cess in the Electronic Credit Ledger (ECL) for items discontinuing cess will be treated. Further clarification or legislative amendments are awaited.
